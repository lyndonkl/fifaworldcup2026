The unit

One dot is money that actually moved: a fixed grain of real money traded at population scale, one trade at zoom scale. Grain changes are narrated on screen, never silent. Dots never stand for scores, statistics, or probabilities; derived quantities are drawn as outline marks above the population.

Data

Tick-level trade history and one-minute candlesticks for every World Cup-related Kalshi market, via the public API; benchmarks from Polymarket minute history, a professional book, published poll waves, and published simulation snapshots. Raw store and reproducible pipeline described in the repository.

The $7.4 billion press figure was Kalshi's own running total from around June 30, 2026, republished by outlets about a week later on July 8. Every headline dollar refreezes at the deploy-morning pipeline run.
  6. The tournament clock: hour-of-day pulse and kickoff-bracketing match windows, in Eastern Time (R11). The schedule's credit is a null model, not a regression: each of the 93 fixtures with a known kickoff time (of 102 total) gets a roughly 3.5-hour window centered on its kickoff. Every clock hour is credited a share of the tournament's total dollar pool in proportion to how many window-minutes fall inside it, so summed across all 24 hours the credit equals the money exactly. The waking-hours figure (about 1.2 times) is money divided by credit, summed across 8 a.m. to 11 p.m. Eastern. No independent attention benchmark exists to test the residual against rival explanations: Google Trends pulls were blocked for this project, so the reading is a clean arithmetic fact, not a proven cause.
  7. Volume concentration and the Lorenz curve: per-market totals with pooled and within-family Gini (R15).
  8. Novelty versus sports order statistics: mention-market ranks placed against the sports markets (R14).
  9. Mexico-England advancement zoom: market summary and the advancement trade tape (R8).
  10. In-play size and arrival-rate regimes, measured against the leg's own lifetime baseline (R16).
  11. Post-goal mechanism across venues: Kalshi reaction latency and matched events, with Pinnacle suspension and Polymarket minute-resolution limits (R3). No cross-venue speed ranking is claimed; sub-minute ordering is unresolvable by construction (R23).
  12. Overreaction and fade: post-jump levels held within the tick-and-fee friction band at a thirty-minute horizon (R20). Whether traders tried to fade the move is a separate question, checked directly against taker-side flow: of the 82 matched goal-reaction events, one round-tripped back to its pre-goal price and was excluded from this measure; across the remaining 81, the share of money in the ten minutes after the goal that bet against the just-made move ran from 27 percent at the 25th percentile to 60 percent at the 75th, with a median of 47 percent.
  13. Germany-Paraguay instrument switch: regulation-leg expiry decay against the advancement leg trading on through the shootout (R4). Kalshi's own contract rules settle the regulation ticket "after 90 minutes plus stoppage time (does not include extra time or penalties)"; a level score at that whistle pays the Tie leg and pays no on the win-in-90 leg.
